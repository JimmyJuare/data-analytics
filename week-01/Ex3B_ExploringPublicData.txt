a) Try searching for butter, or any of your favorite foods. What kind of results do you get? What happens when you click on a result? 
After clicking “Visit Page” from the FoodData Central catalog entry, I arrived at the actual website: https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/.
When I searched for “butter”, the site returned a long list of different types of butter and butter-related products, such as regular butter, salted butter, unsalted butter, ghee, and various branded versions. Each search result displayed basic information including the food description, data type (like Foundation Foods, SR Legacy, or Branded Foods), and key nutrients.
Clicking on a specific result, for example “Butter, salted”, opened a detailed nutrient profile page. This page included a full breakdown of calories, fat, protein, vitamins, and minerals, along with portion sizes, weights and measures, data source information, and sometimes footnotes or references.
The same detailed view appears for any favorite food searched. Overall, the site provides a rich and detailed nutrient profile for each food item.

b) Go back to the home page. Find Foundation Foods, and click on Downloads. What kind of data can you find on this page? How big are these files? Where can you find more information on the data types available?
On the FoodData Central homepage, I navigated to the Foundation Foods section and clicked on Downloads. This page provides access to downloadable data files.
These files contain large, detailed datasets in formats such as CSV, JSON, or zipped packages. They include food descriptions, detailed nutrient values, weights and measures, and metadata for thousands of foods.
The files are often quite large, with some reaching hundreds of megabytes or even over 1 GB when uncompressed. This is because they hold very detailed information on a wide range of food items.
To learn more about the different data types (such as Foundation Foods, SR Legacy, Branded Foods, and FNDDS), I visited the “About Us” section or the documentation and user guide links. The site also offers quick-start videos and fact sheets that explain each data type, update frequency, and key differences.
